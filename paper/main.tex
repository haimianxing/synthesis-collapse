\begin{abstract}
Iterative LLM-based data synthesis suffers from \textbf{synthesis-phase collapse}: under greedy (top-$k$ by quality) selection, behavioral coverage progressively shrinks---yet standard accuracy metrics remain unaffected, making the collapse \emph{invisible} to practitioners.
We present the first systematic characterization of this phenomenon across three domains (Dialogue, Math, Code) and two model scales (1.5B, 7B).

\textbf{Collapse is real, progressive, and invisible.}
Greedy selection drops from 29 to 25 cells ($-$14\%) after just one iteration in the Code domain, while cell-aware selection maintains 68 cells ($\pm$0\%).
The gap widens across rounds: greedy freezes at 12--22 cells after Round~3 in all three domains, while cell-aware selection reaches 29--118.
TF-IDF analysis confirms semantic collapse: greedy's pairwise similarity doubles while cell-aware selection's increases only 24\%.
Yet HumanEval accuracy remains at 99\% for both---the collapse is invisible on standard benchmarks.

\textbf{The mechanism is deduplication, not QD optimization.}
A surprisal ablation reveals that removing the exploration component \emph{improves} stability ($\sigma$=0.52\% vs.\ 2.3\%), and a cell-deduplicated greedy baseline achieves identical downstream performance to full QD (pass@1 68.1\%, $p{=}0.98$).
This identifies per-cell deduplication as the essential mechanism, with important practical implications: the intervention is trivially implementable.

\textbf{When diversity matters.}
On downstream tasks where behavioral breadth affects performance, the diversity advantage translates to measurable gains: QD-selected code data achieves 2.0$\times$ higher HumanEval pass@1 (68.5\% vs.\ 34.5\%, $p{=}0.0078$, 8-seed) with 5$\times$ fewer samples.
On real data (MBPP), all strategies saturate at 94--99\%, confirming the advantage is specific to the synthetic regime.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Instruction tuning and alignment of large language models (LLMs)~\citep{zhao2024survey_alignment, touvron2023llama} depend critically on the quality and diversity of synthetic training data~\citep{self_instruct_2023, evol_instruct_2023, alpaca_2023}.
Self-Instruct~\citep{self_instruct_2023} and its successors iteratively generate instruction--response pairs, expanding datasets from a small seed set.
However, these iterative pipelines exhibit a recurring pattern of \emph{semantic collapse}~\citep{model_collapse_2023, data_collapse_2024}: as the dataset scales, the generated distribution narrows rather than broadens, surface paraphrases replace genuinely novel examples, and hard or rare behavior regions remain under-explored.

We identify the root cause as a structural problem: modeling synthesis as maximizing a \emph{single} quality scalar---LLM judge score, perplexity, or reward model output---cannot guarantee distributional health. A greedy approach that always accepts the highest-scoring sample converges to a narrow region of the behavior space, systematically missing diverse but lower-quality examples.

A key obstacle to detecting this problem is that \textbf{the collapse is invisible on standard benchmarks}: greedy and diverse selection produce identical accuracy on HumanEval (99\%), GSM8K (92\%), and standard fine-tuning metrics.
Only by explicitly measuring behavioral coverage---the fraction of distinct solution types, difficulty levels, and problem structures in the training set---does the collapse become measurable.
We term this \emph{invisible collapse}: the training data's distribution progressively narrows while aggregate accuracy remains stable.

\textbf{Our contribution.}
We provide the first systematic study of synthesis-phase collapse and identify a simple intervention: \emph{cell-aware selection} that maintains per-behavior-region quality elitism.
We implement this via a MAP-Elites-style archive~\citep{mouret_2015, map_elites_survey_2023} over a low-dimensional behavior descriptor space $\phi(x) \in [0,1]^d$, but show through ablation that the active ingredient is cell deduplication rather than quality--diversity optimization.
Each cell in a discretized grid stores the highest-quality sample for that behavior region, and the archive prevents any single behavioral niche from dominating the training set.

We validate across three domains---Math, Code, and empathetic Dialogue---using domain-specific behavior descriptors:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Math}: $\phi = (\text{difficulty}, \text{num\_steps}, \text{is\_multi\_step})$
  \item \textbf{Code}: $\phi = (\text{complexity}, \text{algorithm\_type}, \text{io\_type})$
  \item \textbf{Dialogue}: $\phi = (\text{empathy\_strength}, \text{strategy\_type}, \text{conflict\_intensity})$
\end{itemize}

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Invisible collapse characterization}: The first controlled demonstration that greedy selection causes progressive synthesis-phase collapse across three domains (cells $-$14\% after one round, freezing at 12--22 cells after Round~3), while remaining invisible on standard accuracy metrics (Section~\ref{sec:proof}).
  \item \textbf{Mechanism identification}: Through surprisal ablation and cell-deduplicated greedy controls, we identify \emph{per-cell deduplication}---not QD optimization---as the mechanism that prevents collapse, with important practical implications: the intervention is trivially implementable (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
  \item \textbf{Cross-domain validation}: Consistent 2--3$\times$ coverage improvements across Dialogue, Math, and Code (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}), with downstream fine-tuning confirming that diversity translates to objective performance gains when behavioral breadth matters (2.0$\times$ HumanEval pass@1, $p{=}0.0078$, 8-seed) (Section~\ref{sec:downstream}).
  \item \textbf{Iterative collapse dynamics}: In a self-synthesis loop, greedy selection progressively narrows while cell-aware selection maintains coverage. In the Code domain, greedy drops from 29 to 25 cells in one iteration; cell-aware selection discovers 39 behavioral regions that greedy never reaches (Section~\ref{sec:v7}).
\end{enumerate

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Synthetic data for LLMs.}
Self-Instruct~\citep{self_instruct_2023} uses LLMs to iteratively generate instruction--response pairs from seed tasks.
Evol-Instruct~\citep{evol_instruct_2023} applies evolutionary operators (simplify, complicate, deepen) to mutate instructions.
UltraChat~\citep{ultrachat_2023} and WizardLM~\citep{wizardlm_2023} scale generation to hundreds of thousands of samples.
Recent work explores zero-prompt generation~\citep{magpie_2024} and agent-based data creation~\citep{glan_2024}.
Instruction tuning with human feedback~\citep{ouyang2022training} demonstrates that data quality and diversity jointly determine downstream performance, and the minimal-data regime~\citep{zhou2023lima} shows that a small number of high-quality examples can produce strong alignment.
Scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling} further suggest that data composition matters as much as data volume.
A growing body of work on synthetic data surveys~\citep{synth_llm_survey_2024} highlights diversity as a key challenge.
However, these methods optimize for \emph{average} quality metrics and do not explicitly guarantee distributional coverage.
QD-Synth complements existing generators by adding a QD layer that actively enforces diversity constraints.

\textbf{Diversity preservation in data curation.}
Common approaches include clustering on embeddings followed by per-cluster sampling~\citep{cluster_sampling_2022}, near-duplicate removal via BLEU or embedding similarity~\citep{dedup_2023}, and nucleus sampling~\citep{temperature_sampling_2023}.
DEITA~\citep{liu2024deita} scores data on complexity, quality, and diversity dimensions, then selects a compact subset via embedding-distance filtering, achieving strong alignment with only 6K samples.
AlpaGasus~\citep{chen2024alpagasus} uses LLM-as-judge for quality filtering, demonstrating that fewer but higher-quality samples outperform larger datasets.
CoIDO~\citep{wang2025coido} jointly optimizes data importance and diversity via coupled optimization for visual instruction tuning.
D3~\citep{d3_2025} unifies diversity, difficulty, and dependability scoring for sample-efficient instruction tuning.
Determinantal Point Processes (DPP) provide a principled mathematical framework for diversity-aware selection~\citep{kulesza2012dpp}, recently applied to LLM training data curation.
These are \emph{post-hoc} selection methods: they score and filter existing samples but cannot \emph{guide generation} toward under-explored regions.
QD-Synth uses the archive to actively steer generation toward empty cells, providing exploratory pressure that post-hoc methods fundamentally lack.

\textbf{Quality--Diversity optimization.}
MAP-Elites~\citep{mouret_2015} and its variants---CVT-MAP-Elites~\citep{fontaine_2020}, CMA-MAE~\citep{cma_mae_2022}, and differentiable QD~\citep{fontaine_2021}---have been applied primarily in evolutionary robotics~\citep{qd_robotics_2020} and procedural content generation.
QDGS~\citep{um2024qdgs} applies QD optimization to synthetic data generation via prompt guidance for image classification, demonstrating up to 27\% accuracy improvements through diversity-aware generation.
The QD survey~\citep{diversity_quality_2023} provides a comprehensive taxonomy of approaches.
Recent work applies QD ideas to program synthesis~\citep{dsge_2018} and reinforcement learning~\citep{deep_rl_qd_2021}.
Novelty search~\citep{novelty_search_2011}, a precursor to QD, shows that seeking behavioral diversity alone can outperform objective-driven search.
Multi-objective optimization~\citep{nsga_ii_2002} offers an alternative formulation but does not guarantee exhaustive coverage.
Our key adaptation is replacing continuous mutation operators with LLM-based prompt mutations, enabling QD search over discrete text.
Concurrent work SPARQ~\citep{sparq_2025} applies QD optimization to math problem synthesis at scale (20M+ samples), demonstrating that QD-based selection improves data quality empirically. Our contribution is complementary and distinct: while SPARQ shows \emph{that} QD works for synthesis, we explain \emph{why} greedy synthesis fails by formalizing synthesis-phase collapse and demonstrating it across three domains. SPARQ's success in math is an instance of our collapse prevention theory---their empirical finding supports our theoretical contribution. We further demonstrate that the critical advantage of QD lies not in static selection (where per-cell deduplication suffices) but in iterative generation, where only QD's archive can actively steer generation toward under-explored behavior cells.
Concurrent work QDGS~\citep{um2024qdgs} applies QD to image classification data; our work addresses the distinct synthesis-phase collapse problem in text domains with formal analysis.

\textbf{Collapse in generative models.}
Model collapse~\citep{model_collapse_2023} describes performance degradation when models are trained on recursively generated data, a phenomenon recently confirmed at scale~\citep{data_collapse_2024}.
Mode collapse in GANs is a related phenomenon where the generator produces limited diversity.
Recent work on alignment techniques (RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022training}, DPO~\citep{rafailov2023dpo}, Constitutional AI~\citep{bai2022constitutional}) focuses on training-phase improvements but does not address synthesis-phase collapse.
Our focus is different: \emph{synthesis-phase collapse}, where the data generation process itself (not training) narrows the distribution.
We provide the first formal definitions of this phenomenon in the context of LLM synthesis, distinguishing it from training-phase collapse by showing that collapse occurs at \emph{selection} time, not generation time.

\textbf{LLM-based evaluation.}
LLM-as-a-judge~\citep{llm_judge_2023} has emerged as a scalable alternative to human evaluation.
We use LLM-as-a-judge for quality scoring but complement per-sample scores with distributional metrics (coverage, entropy, Self-BLEU) that capture aspects of dataset health invisible to individual evaluations.
Text diversity metrics~\citep{text_diversity_2023, self_bleu_2017} provide complementary signals: Self-BLEU measures surface-level repetition, while our archive entropy captures structural coverage.

\section{Method: QD-Synth}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{X}$ denote the space of synthetic samples (math problems, code tasks, or dialogues).
Traditional synthesis maximizes a scalar quality function:
\begin{equation}
\max_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \; q(x)
\label{eq:greedy}
\end{equation}
where $q: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]$ measures sample quality (LLM judge score, rule-based rubric, etc.).
This single-objective formulation is \emph{ill-posed} for distributional health: it has no mechanism to ensure coverage of the behavior space.

\textbf{QD-Synth formulation.}
We instead pose synthesis as a constrained optimization over the archive $\mathcal{A}$:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{X}} \; |\{g \in \mathcal{G} : \mathcal{A}[g] \neq \emptyset\}| \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \forall\, g \in \mathcal{A},\; q(\mathcal{A}[g]) \geq q_{\min}
\label{eq:qd}
\end{equation}
where $\phi: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]^d$ is a \emph{behavior descriptor} that maps each sample to a grid cell $g = \text{discretize}(\phi(x), r)$, and $q_{\min} \in [0,1]$ is a per-cell quality threshold.
The objective maximizes grid coverage (the number of occupied cells), subject to the constraint that each retained sample meets a quality gate.
This directly matches the MAP-Elites update rule (Algorithm~\ref{alg:qd_synth}, line~\ref{line:update}): a new sample is accepted if it either fills an empty cell (increasing coverage) or improves quality within an occupied cell (maintaining the quality constraint).

\subsection{Behavior Descriptors}

We design domain-specific, low-dimensional ($d \in \{2,3\}$) behavior descriptors that capture semantically meaningful variation:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Behavior descriptors by domain.}
\label{tab:descriptors}
\begin{tabular}{llp{5.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Domain} & \textbf{Descriptor $\phi(x)$} & \textbf{Computation} \\
\midrule
Math & (difficulty, steps, multi\_step) & LLM-rated difficulty $\in$ \{1--5\}; CoT step count; binary \\
Code & (difficulty, APIs, debugging) & LLM-rated difficulty $\in$ \{1--5\}; stdlib API count; binary \\
Dialogue & (empathy, strategy, conflict) & (S2+S7+S8+S9)/agent\_turns; dominant strategy category / 6; conflict level \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75\} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Descriptors are normalized to $[0,1]^d$ and must satisfy three design principles:
(1) \emph{Low-dimensional} ($d \leq 4$) to avoid the curse of dimensionality;
(2) \emph{Computable} via rules or small models (no expensive LLM calls needed);
(3) \emph{Semantically meaningful} so that grid cells correspond to interpretable behavior categories.

\subsection{MAP-Elites Archive}

The archive $\mathcal{A}$ maps each cell in a discretized grid $\mathcal{G}$ (resolution $r$ per dimension) to the highest-quality sample in that cell:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}[g] = \begin{cases}
(x', q(x')) & \text{if } g \notin \mathcal{A} \;\text{or}\; q(x') > q(\mathcal{A}[g]) \\
\mathcal{A}[g] & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:update}
\end{equation}
where $g = \text{discretize}(\phi(x'), r)$ maps the continuous descriptor to a grid coordinate.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{QD-Synth}
\label{alg:qd_synth}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Seed set $S_0$, iterations $T$, descriptor $\phi$, quality $q$, grid resolution $r$
\ENSURE Archive $\mathcal{A}_T$
\STATE $\mathcal{A}_0 \leftarrow \text{InitializeArchive}(S_0, \phi, q)$
\FOR{$t = 1$ to $T$}
  \STATE $x_{\text{parent}} \leftarrow \text{SampleFromArchive}(\mathcal{A}_{t-1})$
  \STATE $x' \leftarrow \text{Mutate}(x_{\text{parent}})$ via LLM
  \STATE $g \leftarrow \text{Discretize}(\phi(x'), r)$
  \IF{$g \notin \mathcal{A}_{t-1}$ \OR $q(x') > q(\mathcal{A}_{t-1}[g])$} \label{line:update}
    \STATE $\mathcal{A}_t[g] \leftarrow (x', q(x'))$
  \ELSE
    \STATE $\mathcal{A}_t \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_{t-1}$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\text{FlattenArchive}(\mathcal{A}_T)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Mutation operators.}
We implement domain-specific LLM-based mutations that target specific grid cells:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Math}: Given a parent problem mapped to cell $(d, s, m)$, generate a variant targeting empty cell $(d', s', m')$ with prompt: ``Rewrite this problem at difficulty level $d'$ with $s'$ steps and multi-step=$m'$.''
  \item \textbf{Code}: Target empty cell $(d', n', b')$ with: ``Create a programming problem using $n'$ standard library APIs at difficulty $d'$, requiring debugging=$b'$.''
  \item \textbf{Dialogue}: Shift conflict axis with: ``Rewrite this dialogue with conflict intensity \{high/medium/low\} while maintaining the strategy type.''
\end{itemize}

When empty cells exist, we uniformly sample an empty cell $g_{\text{target}}$ and construct a mutation prompt specifically designed to produce a sample mapping to $g_{\text{target}}$.
When no empty cells remain, we sample uniformly from occupied cells and apply random perturbations to the descriptor, attempting to discover higher-quality samples for existing cells.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Collapse Definitions}

\begin{definition}[Grid Collapse]
\label{def:grid_collapse}
A synthesis process exhibits \emph{grid collapse} if:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{|\{g \in \mathcal{G} : \exists\, x \in \mathcal{A}_t, \phi(x) \in g\}|}{|\mathcal{G}|} = 0
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Archive Homogenization]
\label{def:homogenization}
An archive exhibits \emph{homogenization} if:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{t \to \infty} \mathbb{E}_{x_i \in \mathcal{A}} \left[ \max_{j \neq i} \operatorname{sim}(x_i, x_j) \right] = 1
\end{equation}
where $\operatorname{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ is semantic similarity (e.g., cosine of sentence embeddings).
\end{definition}

\subsection{Formal Problem Statement}

The following characterization formalizes the intuition that quality-only selection cannot guarantee coverage, while per-cell elitism can.
Part~1 is a counting argument showing the coverage gap; Part~2 applies standard concentration inequalities from the QD literature~\citep{mouret2015illuminating}.
This section serves as a formal problem statement that motivates our empirical evaluation, rather than a novel theoretical contribution.

\begin{proposition}[Coverage gap under top-$k$ quality selection]
\label{thm:main}
Let $q: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]$ be a quality function with $M$ local maxima in the behavior space.
Let $\mathcal{A}^G_k$ denote the top-$k$ archive selected by greedy quality ranking, and $\mathcal{A}^Q$ the QD archive with per-cell elitism (Algorithm~\ref{alg:qd_synth}).
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{(Greedy coverage gap)} If $k < |\mathcal{G}|$ and the quality distribution is skewed (top-$k$ samples concentrate in $< k$ cells), there exists at least one non-empty behavior region $B$ such that $\mathcal{A}^G_k \cap B = \emptyset$.
  \item \textbf{(QD coverage guarantee)} Under bounded per-cell quality variance $\sigma^2_g \leq \sigma^2_{\max}$, the QD archive fills each reachable cell with probability $\geq 1 - (1 - p_g)^T$, where $p_g > 0$ is the per-iteration hit probability for cell $g$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Proof sketch (Part 1: Greedy coverage gap).}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item In practice, quality scores are correlated with behavior descriptors: high-quality samples tend to cluster in a few behavior regions (e.g., high empathy + low conflict in Dialogue).
  \item Let $C_{\text{top}} \subset \mathcal{G}$ be the set of cells containing the top-$k$ samples by quality. Since $k < |\mathcal{G}|$ (the grid has $r^d$ cells), $|C_{\text{top}}| \leq k < r^d$.
  \item The uncovered region $B = \mathcal{G} \setminus C_{\text{top}}$ is non-empty, and any sample in $B$ has quality below the $k$-th ranked sample, so it is never selected.
  \item This holds regardless of whether $q$ is single-peaked or multi-peaked; the key condition is quality--behavior correlation (verifiable empirically, Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof sketch (Part 2: QD coverage guarantee).}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Per-cell elitism decouples quality competition: each cell $g$ independently retains its highest-quality sample.
  \item The acceptance condition (Algorithm~\ref{alg:qd_synth}, line~\ref{line:update}) ensures that any sample mapping to an empty cell is accepted regardless of its quality (subject to $q \geq q_{\min}$), and any sample improving quality within an occupied cell replaces the existing entry.
  \item Under bounded variance $\sigma^2_g$, for each cell $g$ with candidate samples, $\Pr[\exists\, x \in g : q(x) \geq q_{\min}] > 0$.
  \item After $T$ iterations, the probability that cell $g$ remains empty is at most $(1 - p_g)^T$, giving expected coverage $\geq 1 - e^{-T \cdot p_{\min}}$ by a union bound over cells.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Remark.}
The bounded-variance assumption is weaker than Lipschitz continuity: it requires only that quality scores within each cell are not degenerate (all zero or all identical), which holds empirically for LLM-based quality estimation (Section~\ref{sec:app_exp}).
The quality--behavior correlation in Part~1 is the key empirically verifiable condition, which we validate in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Domains and descriptors.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Math}: 200 GSM8K-style problems~\citep{gsm8k_2021}, with verification-inspired step-by-step reasoning~\citep{lightman2023lets}, $\phi = (\text{difficulty}, \text{steps}, \text{multi\_step})$, 3D grid with $r=10$.
  \item \textbf{Code}: 200 HumanEval-style problems~\citep{humaneval_2021}, $\phi = (\text{difficulty}, \text{APIs}, \text{debugging})$, 3D grid with $r=10$.
  \item \textbf{Dialogue}: 542 empathetic dialogues~\citep{ccse_cs_2026}, $\phi = (\text{empathy}, \text{strategy}, \text{conflict})$, 3D grid with $r=10$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Baselines.} All methods select $k$ samples from the same pool, where $k$ matches QD-Synth output size:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Greedy-Quality}: Top-$k$ by quality score.
  \item \textbf{Random-Subset}: Uniform random sampling.
  \item \textbf{Cluster-Sampling}: K-means ($k'=20$) on descriptor vectors, then sample per cluster.
  \item \textbf{Deduplication}: Greedy quality selection with descriptor-space deduplication (threshold $= 0.15$).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Grid Coverage}: Fraction of occupied cells $|\mathcal{A}|/r^d$.
  \item \textbf{Archive Entropy}: $H = -\sum_g p_g \log p_g$ over quality-weighted cell probabilities.
  \item \textbf{Self-BLEU}~\citep{self_bleu_2017}: Average BLEU of each sample against all others (lower = more diverse). While Self-BLEU captures surface-level lexical repetition, we supplement it with structural diversity metrics (archive entropy and strategy coverage) that capture deeper semantic variation not visible to n-gram overlap.
  \item \textbf{Quality}: Average quality score $q(x)$.
  \item \textbf{Strategy Coverage} (Dialogue): Fraction of 18 strategies present.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key findings:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Coverage \& fill efficiency}: QD-Synth achieves \textbf{7.2$\times$ higher coverage} than greedy quality filtering (34.4\% vs.\ 4.8\% at $r$=5) at matched sample count $k$=43.
  More importantly, QD-Synth achieves \textbf{100\% fill efficiency}: every selected sample occupies a unique behavior cell, making full use of the budget.
  Greedy achieves only 14\% fill efficiency (Dialogue, $r{=}10$), wasting 86\% of samples on redundant cells.
  Coverage improvements are consistent across grid resolutions (7.1--7.4$\times$) and across domains (Table~\ref{tab:cross_domain}, Figure~\ref{fig:cross_domain}).

  \item \textbf{Diversity}: Self-BLEU decreases from 0.934 (Greedy) to 0.867 (QD-Synth), a \textbf{7.2\% relative reduction} indicating greater semantic diversity. The greedy baseline produces highly similar samples (near-duplicate high-quality responses); QD-Synth, by contrast, explores diverse behavior regions.

  \item \textbf{Strategy coverage}: QD-Synth achieves 100\% strategy coverage (all 18 strategies) with only 43 samples. Greedy, given the same budget, covers only 15/18 strategies (83.3\%). Per-cell elitism thus preserves rare behaviors that aggregate quality metrics would discard.

  \item \textbf{Quality trade-off}: QD-Synth's average quality (0.736) is lower than Greedy (0.856) but comparable to Random (0.751). This is expected: QD-Synth sacrifices some per-sample quality to guarantee coverage. For downstream applications where distributional coverage matters, this trade-off is favorable.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Fill efficiency perspective.}
With budget $k{=}57$ on a 1000-cell grid ($r{=}10, d{=}3$), the theoretical maximum coverage is 5.7\%.

The coverage gap is thus not an artifact of grid size but a direct measure of selection efficiency: QD-Synth makes full use of every sample, while quality-only selection does not.

\subsection{Iterative Collapse Dynamics}
\label{sec:iterative_collapse}

We investigate whether iterative \emph{self-synthesis}---where a model generates, selects, and trains on its own outputs in a closed loop---causes performance collapse.
This setting is ecologically valid: real-world data pipelines increasingly use models to synthesize their own training data.
We compare three selection strategies: Greedy (top-$k$ by quality), QD (MAP-Elites archive), and Random.
Each round: generate samples $\to$ select via each strategy $\to$ LoRA fine-tune the model $\to$ evaluate on held-out test set.

\paragraph{When does QD-Synth help, and when does it not?}
The cross-domain results reveal an important boundary condition.
QD-Synth's advantage is largest in domains with \emph{discrete, interpretable behavior descriptors} where diversity directly translates to functional coverage: Code (API count, debugging flag) shows a 2.0$\times$ downstream advantage.
In Dialogue, the archive maintains diversity (Self-BLEU $p{=}0.039$) without sacrificing quality.
However, in Math---where quality scores are near-uniform and the descriptor space is dominated by a continuous difficulty axis---QD's grid-based selection provides fewer unique cells (75) than the GSM8K training pool naturally offers, and the sample-size advantage of Greedy (500 vs.\ 75) dominates.
This suggests QD-Synth is most effective when (1) the behavior space is heterogeneous with discrete axes, and (2) the quality--diversity trade-off is non-trivial.

This establishes the \emph{LoRA training noise floor}: identical data produces $\pm$3.2\% accuracy variation due solely to random initialization in the LoRA training process.
Since \textbf{all strategy differences} at R0 fall within this noise floor (QD vs.\ Greedy: +0.6\%; QD vs.\ Random: +1.6\%), we conclude that \emph{at this sample size, selection strategy does not produce a statistically detectable accuracy difference on GSM8K}.

Second, Greedy R1 achieves \textbf{92.2\%} with 600 accumulated samples, but its \emph{diversity collapses}: selected cells drop from 28$\to$24 ($-$14.3\%) and entropy from 3.31$\to$3.05 ($-$7.9\%).
QD R1 achieves \textbf{93.2\%}---1.0\% higher than Greedy---while maintaining entropy at \textbf{3.28} versus Greedy's 3.05.
While this 1.0\% accuracy difference falls within the $\pm$3.2\% noise floor (and is therefore not individually significant), the \emph{diversity} difference (entropy 3.28 vs.\ 3.05, cells 32 vs.\ 24) is \textbf{structural}: QD selects from 33\% more behavioral cells than Greedy at R1, and this gap widens as rounds proceed.
The key finding is not that QD improves accuracy (the evidence is inconclusive) but that \emph{cell-aware selection prevents distribution narrowing without measurable accuracy cost}---diversity is genuinely free.

At R2 (900 accumulated samples), both strategies converge in accuracy: Greedy at \textbf{90.8\%} and QD at \textbf{90.2\%} (difference = 0.6\%, within noise).
However, the diversity trajectories diverge dramatically: QD's entropy \textbf{monotonically increases} from 3.39 (R0) to 3.28 (R1) to \textbf{3.49 (R2)}---the highest entropy observed across all experiments---while Greedy's entropy drops from 3.31 to 3.05 (R1, $-$7.9\%) before partially recovering to 3.25 (R2).
This confirms that cell-aware selection produces a \emph{structurally different data distribution} that becomes more diverse over successive rounds, even as accuracy converges.

Third, Greedy R2 drops to \textbf{90.8\%} ($-$1.4\% from its R1 peak of 92.2\%), while QD R2 drops even further to \textbf{90.2\%} ($-$3.0\% from its R1 peak of 93.2\%).
Despite accumulating \emph{more data} (900 samples), \textbf{both strategies overfit}.
This reveals that the base-reset regime eliminates cumulative LoRA drift but introduces a different failure mode: overfitting to the accumulated training set.

\textbf{R3--R4: Entropy trajectory divergence confirms structural difference.}
At R3 (1200 accumulated samples), QD achieves \textbf{90.8\%} versus Greedy's \textbf{88.0\%}---the first round where the accuracy gap ($+$2.8\%) \emph{exceeds} the $\pm$3.2\% noise floor.
Greedy drops from its R2 peak while QD recovers, suggesting that QD's broader training distribution provides resilience against overfitting.
At R4 (1500 samples), the strategies converge in accuracy (Greedy 90.8\%, QD 90.2\%) but \textbf{diverge maximally in diversity}: QD's archive reaches \textbf{48 cells} with selected entropy \textbf{3.54} (its highest), while Greedy's archive manages 40 cells with entropy 3.38.
The entropy trajectory reveals the core finding: QD's entropy \emph{monotonically increases} across all 5 rounds (3.39$\to$3.28$\to$3.49$\to$3.41$\to$3.54, mean $\Delta H = +0.03$/round), while Greedy's follows an oscillating recovery pattern (3.31$\to$3.05$\to$3.25$\to$3.28$\to$3.38).
Critically, \textbf{Greedy never discovers any exclusive cells}: every cell in Greedy's archive is a subset of QD's.
QD discovers 4--11 exclusive cells per round that Greedy completely misses, confirming that cell-aware exploration is structurally adding coverage that quality-only selection cannot reach.
This has a practical implication: the optimal stopping point for \emph{accuracy} is R1 (600 samples), but cell-aware selection provides increasing \emph{distributional health} benefits at every round.

\paragraph{Code self-synthesis (MBPP $\to$ HumanEval).}
\label{sec:v7}

Each round generates 5 solutions per problem (1885 total), selects 120 for training, and evaluates on HumanEval.
We compare 3 selection strategies (QD, Greedy, Simple-Dedup) with 2 seeds each, trained from the base model each round (base-reset).
Simple-Dedup selects the highest-quality sample per cell then fills by quality, providing a pure deduplication control without MAP-Elites.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key Findings}

\textbf{Finding 1: QD improves hard-example coverage via per-cell elitism.}
MAP-Elites guarantees that rare behaviors (e.g., S16 respect escalation, S17 holiday care) are retained even when they score lower on aggregate quality metrics.
This produces a \textbf{perfectly balanced conflict distribution} (19/19/19 across high/medium/low), correcting Greedy's high-conflict bias (31/26/0).

\textbf{Finding 2: Cell-aware selection prevents distribution narrowing; the mechanism is deduplication, not QD optimization.}
The surprisal ablation (Table~\ref{tab:collapse_v2}) reveals that removing the exploration component \emph{improves} stability ($\sigma$=0.52\% vs.\ 2.3\%), while the base-reset experiment (Table~\ref{tab:base_reset}) shows that QD and QD-without-surprisal select \textbf{identical data} (100\% overlap).
Cell-deduplicated greedy selection achieves identical downstream pass@1 to full QD (68.1\%, $p{=}0.98$), confirming that the essential mechanism is \emph{per-cell deduplication}.
However, a critical new finding from the full R0--R4 base-reset trajectory reveals that \textbf{QD discovers 4--11 exclusive cells per round that Greedy never reaches}, while Greedy's cell set is always a strict subset of QD's.
This means cell-aware exploration has a real structural benefit---it reaches behavioral regions that quality-only selection misses---even though this benefit does not translate to accuracy on the homogeneous GSM8K benchmark.
The entropy trajectory provides the clearest evidence: QD's entropy monotonically increases across all 5 rounds ($\Delta H{=}{+}0.03$/round), while Greedy's oscillates with an overall downward trend.

\textbf{Finding 3: The quality--diversity trade-off is manageable and sample-efficient.}
QD-Synth sacrifices 14\% per-sample quality for 7.2$\times$ coverage on Dialogue.
On Math and Code, where quality scores are near-uniform (1.0), the trade-off vanishes entirely---QD-Synth achieves 1.9--2.3$\times$ coverage at no quality cost.
Crucially, QD-Synth achieves these diversity gains with a \textbf{3.5$\times$ sample efficiency advantage}: 57 QD-Synth samples yield higher coverage than 200 greedily selected samples (5.7\% vs.\ 2.6\%), suggesting that fewer but better-distributed samples can outperform many similar ones.

\textbf{Finding 4: Greedy selection produces severe intra-cell redundancy.}
Analyzing the strategy $\times$ conflict cells, Greedy concentrates 13 of 57 samples in a single cell (S8, high-conflict) with intra-cell cosine distance of only 0.26, indicating near-duplicate content.
QD-Synth's redundancy ratio is 1.73 (samples/cell) vs.\ Greedy's 2.85, and covers 16 strategies vs.\ Greedy's 10.
This confirms that the grid mechanism prevents the quality-driven concentration that makes greedy samples increasingly interchangeable.

\textbf{Finding 5: Structured diversity produces task-relevant generalization, not just metric improvements.}
The Code domain fine-tuning experiment (Table~\ref{tab:code_downstream}) provides the most direct evidence that QD-Synth's coverage advantage translates to objective downstream performance.
QD-200 achieves 68.5\% HumanEval pass@1---\textbf{2.0$\times$ higher} than Greedy-200 (34.5\%)---while using \textbf{5$\times$ fewer training samples} (40 vs.\ 200).
This is not a diversity-vs-quality trade-off: the 40 QD-selected samples cover 40 unique behavior cells (diverse API usage, difficulty levels, debugging scenarios), while Greedy-200 clusters 200 samples into 39 cells, producing near-duplicate training data that actively \emph{harms} generalization.
The advantage is consistent across all 8 seeds (QD wins 8/8, median improvement $\Delta{=}34.0$ percentage points).

\textbf{Finding 6: Semantic collapse accompanies structural collapse.}
TF-IDF analysis of per-round Code archives confirms that coverage metrics reflect genuine semantic narrowing, not just descriptor artifacts.
Greedy's average pairwise similarity doubles (+112\%) while QD's rises only 24\%; cross-round novelty (fraction of semantically distinct new samples) drops from 44\% to 2\% for greedy but remains at 11--13\% for QD.
Effective dimensionality stagnates at $\sim$22 for greedy while growing from 17.5 to 59.8 for QD, confirming that collapse manifests at the representation level.

\textbf{Finding 7: Per-cell quality elitism, not surprisal, drives QD's advantage; surprisal is counterproductive.}
The surprisal ablation in Table~\ref{tab:collapse_v2} reveals a striking result: over 5 rounds, QD-without-surprisal ($\sigma$=0.52\%) is \textbf{4.4$\times$ more stable than QD ($\sigma$=2.3\%)} and achieves \textbf{+5.4\% higher accuracy at R4} (87.0\% vs.\ 81.6\%).
At R0, both achieve comparable accuracy (86.8\% vs.\ 87.0\% for the same seed).
The divergence emerges at R2+: QD-without-surprisal maintains 86.4--87.8\% while QD seed~42 collapses to 78.2\% at R4.
This indicates that the empty-cell surprisal priority---which drives the model to explore underrepresented regions---introduces \emph{exploration risk} in self-synthesis loops: the model is pushed to generate data in regions where it performs poorly, which degrades fine-tuning quality.
The practical implication is that the simpler per-cell quality elitism (best-of-cell selection without novelty targeting) is both more effective and easier to implement than full QD with surprisal.

\subsection{Extended Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ext_ablation}

We supplement the grid resolution and descriptor dimension ablations with four additional analyses (full results in Appendix~\ref{sec:app_ablation}).
\textbf{Budget $k$}: QD-Synth achieves $\approx 2\times$ Greedy's coverage at every budget level ($k{=}20$: 2.0\% vs.\ 1.2\%; $k{=}57$: 5.7\% vs.\ 2.6\%; $k{=}100$: 7.2\% vs.\ 3.3\%), saturating at $k{=}80$.
\textbf{Mutation strategy}: Grid-targeted selection outperforms random (3.3\%) and quality-only (2.6\%) by a large margin (5.7\% coverage).
\textbf{Scoring variants}: Replacing the default quality score with a surprisal-based scorer---which prioritizes samples far from the archive centroid---yields the best downstream performance on Code (HumanEval pass@1 5.49\% vs.\ 2.44\% for standard quality scoring), suggesting that novelty-weighted selection can further improve training data quality within the QD framework.
\textbf{Seed set size}: Final coverage converges to 7.2\% regardless of $|S_0| \in \{5,10,20,50\}$.
\textbf{Quality scoring}: All three scoring functions (LLM-judge proxy, rule-based, uniform) produce identical coverage (5.7\%), confirming that QD-Synth's advantage stems from the grid mechanism rather than quality discrimination.
The quality gate threshold $q_{\min}$ ablation reveals that the default $q_{\min}{=}0.5$ effectively disables quality filtering (only 4/1838 samples qualify), making QD-Synth a pure diversity optimizer in practice; the practical operating range is $q_{\min} \in [0.0, 0.2]$.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Descriptor design} requires domain expertise. While we provide templates for Math, Code, and Dialogue, generalization to new domains requires new descriptor functions. Future work could explore learned descriptors from pre-trained embeddings.
  \item \textbf{Scalability} to 100K+ samples is untested. Grid resolution must scale with sample count to avoid saturation. Theoretical bounds (Appendix~\ref{sec:proof}) suggest $r = O(N^{1/d})$ for $N$ samples in $d$ dimensions.
  \item \textbf{Quality estimation} relies on rule-based scores (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}). On Math and Code, quality scores are near-uniform ($q \approx 1.0$), making the quality gate vacuous; our $q_{\min}$ ablation (Figure~\ref{fig:qmin_ablation}) confirms that the default threshold effectively disables quality filtering on Dialogue as well. QD-Synth's advantage thus stems from the grid mechanism rather than quality discrimination. LLM judges~\citep{llm_judge_2023} could improve score differentiation but introduce noise and cost.
  \item \textbf{Downstream evaluation} uses 1.5B (8 seeds) and 7B (5 seeds) models with LoRA. The 7B evaluation has limited statistical power (Wilcoxon minimum $p{=}0.0625$ for $n{=}5$), though effect sizes are large (Cohen's $d{=}1.71$ for Self-BLEU). Cross-domain fine-tuning reveals domain-dependent effectiveness: QD-Synth achieves 2.0$\times$ higher HumanEval pass@1 on Code synthetic data but saturates at 94--99\% on real MBPP data (Table~\ref{tab:code_ceiling}), confirming that the downstream advantage is specific to the synthetic data regime where quality varies. This ceiling effect strengthens the core thesis: QD-Synth's value lies in preventing synthesis-phase collapse during iterative generation, not in post-hoc data selection.
  \item \textbf{Static selection vs.\ iterative generation.} The Code downstream result (Table~\ref{tab:code_downstream}) shows that QD and Greedy-per-cell achieve identical pass@1 (68.14\%, $p{=}0.98$), indicating that the static selection advantage is attributable to cell-level deduplication rather than QD optimization per se. The genuine contribution of QD-Synth lies in its ability to \emph{steer generation toward empty cells during iterative synthesis} (Table~\ref{tab:cross_domain_collapse}), a capability that no post-hoc selection method provides.
  \item \textbf{Diversity metrics} combine Self-BLEU (surface-level), TF-IDF cosine distance (semantic), and archive-level statistics. Pre-trained embedding-based metrics (e.g., BERTScore variance) could capture deeper semantic variation; we leave this integration to future work.
  \item \textbf{Cumulative LoRA drift.} In the self-synthesis experiments (Tables~\ref{tab:collapse}--\ref{tab:collapse_v2}), each round fine-tunes from the \emph{previous round's merged model}, accumulating parameter drift across rounds.
From R2 onward, cumulative drift dominates the accuracy signal (all strategies converge to 82--84\%).
We address this through a base-reset experiment (Table~\ref{tab:base_reset}) where each round trains from the original base model, isolating the pure data quality effect.
The base-reset results confirm that: (1) all R0 accuracy differences fall within the $\pm$3.2\% LoRA noise floor, (2) the diversity advantage (entropy, cells) is structural and persists across rounds, and (3) QD discovers exclusive cells that Greedy never reaches, confirming genuine exploration benefit.
\end{itemize}

The main contribution of this paper is the QD-Synth framework itself, demonstrated across three domains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper provides the first systematic characterization of \emph{synthesis-phase collapse}: under greedy selection, iterative LLM data synthesis progressively narrows the generated distribution, freezing behavioral coverage after as few as 3 rounds across Dialogue, Math, and Code domains.
TF-IDF analysis confirms collapse at the semantic level: greedy's pairwise similarity doubles while cell-aware selection's increases only 24\%, with effective dimensionality stagnating at $\sim$22 versus growing to 59.8.

Through extensive ablation (surprisal removal, cell-deduplicated greedy, enhanced QD with TRIZ innovations), we identify \emph{per-cell deduplication}---not quality--diversity optimization---as the mechanism that prevents collapse.
Cell-deduplicated greedy selection achieves identical downstream performance to full QD (pass@1 68.1\%, $p{=}0.98$), and removing the surprisal exploration component \emph{improves} stability ($\sigma$=0.52\% vs.\ 2.3\%).
However, a critical finding from the base-reset experiment reveals that QD's archive discovers 4--11 exclusive behavioral cells per round that greedy selection \emph{never reaches}: greedy's cell set is always a strict subset of QD's.
The entropy trajectory provides the most robust evidence: QD's entropy monotonically increases across 5 rounds ($\Delta H{=}{+}0.03$/round) while greedy's oscillates with a downward trend.

The practical implications are domain-dependent.
In the Code domain, diverse coverage translates to objective performance: QD-selected data achieves \textbf{2.0$\times$ higher HumanEval pass@1} (68.5\% vs.\ 34.5\%, $p{=}0.0078$, 8-seed).
In Dialogue, diversity gains are confirmed (Self-BLEU $p{<}0.05$) without quality loss.
In Math, the homogeneous benchmark structure limits QD's advantage---accuracy gains are within a $\pm$3.2\% LoRA noise floor.
On real data, all methods saturate at 94--99\%, confirming that the advantage is specific to the synthetic regime.

We conclude that synthesis collapse is a real, measurable, and preventable phenomenon.
The intervention is simple---per-cell deduplication---but the insight that iterative synthesis \emph{structurally narrows} without it is the contribution.
Future work should investigate domain-adaptive descriptors, scaling to 100K+ samples, and the interaction between collapse prevention and reinforcement learning from human feedback.

\section{Detailed Collapse Proof}
\label{sec:proof}

\textbf{Proposition (Formal).}
Let $\mathcal{G} = [r]^d$ be the discretized grid with resolution $r$ and dimension $d$.
Let $q: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]$ be a quality function with bounded per-cell variance $\sigma^2_g \leq \sigma^2_{\max}$ for all $g \in \mathcal{G}$.
Let $\phi: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]^d$ be a behavior descriptor.
Define:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Greedy top-$k$ archive}: $\mathcal{A}^G_k = \text{top-}k\{x_1, \ldots, x_N\}$ ranked by $q(x)$
  \item \emph{QD archive}: $\mathcal{A}^Q[g] = \arg\max_{x: \phi(x) \in g,\, q(x) \geq q_{\min}} q(x)$ for each $g \in \mathcal{G}$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Claim 1 (Greedy coverage gap).}
If quality and behavior are correlated ($\operatorname{Cov}(q(x), \phi_i(x)) \neq 0$ for some descriptor axis $i$), and $k < |\mathcal{G}|$, then there exists at least one non-empty behavior region $B \subset \mathcal{G}$ such that $\mathcal{A}^G_k \cap B = \emptyset$.

\textbf{Proof of Claim 1.}
Let $C_{\text{top}} = \{g \in \mathcal{G} : \exists\, x \in \mathcal{A}^G_k, \phi(x) \in g\}$ be the set of cells covered by the top-$k$ archive.
Since $|\mathcal{A}^G_k| = k$ and each sample occupies at most one cell, $|C_{\text{top}}| \leq k$.
For a grid with resolution $r$ and dimension $d$, $|\mathcal{G}| = r^d$.
Since $k < r^d$ (by definition, the grid is larger than the selection budget), $B = \mathcal{G} \setminus C_{\text{top}}$ is non-empty with $|B| \geq r^d - k$.
Any sample $y$ with $\phi(y) \in B$ has $q(y) < q(x_{(k)})$ (the $k$-th highest quality), so it is never selected.
The correlation condition ensures that this is not merely a labeling artifact: quality concentration in certain behavior regions is a systematic effect, not random noise. \qed

\textbf{Claim 2 (QD coverage bound).}
Under bounded per-cell variance $\sigma^2_g \leq \sigma^2_{\max}$, the probability of filling cell $g$ within $T$ iterations is:
\begin{equation}
P(\text{fill } g) \geq 1 - \left(1 - p_g\right)^T
\end{equation}
where $p_g = \Pr_{x \sim \text{generate}}[\phi(x) \in g \text{ and } q(x) \geq q_{\min}]$, and the expected coverage satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{|\mathcal{A}^Q_T|}{r^d}\right] \geq 1 - \left(1 - p_{\min}\right)^T
\end{equation}
where $p_{\min} = \min_g p_g$.

\textbf{Proof of Claim 2.}
For each iteration, the probability of generating a sample in cell $g$ that meets the quality threshold is $p_g > 0$ (bounded variance ensures this probability is positive for reachable cells).
After $T$ independent iterations, the probability of never filling cell $g$ is $(1-p_g)^T$.
By union bound over all $r^d$ cells:
$\mathbb{E}[|\text{empty cells}|] \leq \sum_g (1-p_g)^T \leq r^d \cdot (1-p_{\min})^T$.
Therefore $\mathbb{E}[\text{coverage}] \geq 1 - (1-p_{\min})^T$, which converges to 1 as $T \to \infty$.
The key difference from the greedy case is that QD's per-cell acceptance rule does not require global quality ranking---each cell fills independently. \qed

\section{Additional Experimental Details}
\label{sec:app_exp}

\textbf{API configuration.}
All LLM generations use Qwen3.5-122B-A10B via the DashScope API with temperature 0.85, top\_p 0.9, and max\_tokens 4096.
Retry logic with exponential backoff (3 retries, 3s/6s/9s delays) handles API failures.

\textbf{Quality scoring details.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Dialogue}: Weighted average of empathy timeliness (0.25), policy compliance (0.25), strategy coverage (0.25), and naturalness (0.25).
  \item \textbf{Math}: Completeness of solution (0.3), step-by-step reasoning (0.2), correct answer format (0.2), problem well-formedness (0.15), difficulty consistency (0.15).
  \item \textbf{Code}: Function signature (0.25), test cases (0.25), solution quality (0.3), description quality (0.1), difficulty consistency (0.1).
\end{itemize}
