\begin{abstract}

When does data quality matter for fine-tuning?
We examine this question in the context of \textbf{synthesis-phase collapse}: under greedy selection, behavioral coverage shrinks by 2.4$\times$---yet this collapse is invisible on standard benchmarks in most practical settings.
We derive a \emph{detectability threshold} that predicts three regimes: \textbf{detectable} when evaluation is below ceiling, \textbf{ceiling} when the model saturates, and \textbf{noise} when training stochasticity dominates.
In the noise regime, even an $\infty\times$ correctness difference in training data produces no detectable downstream gap ($p{=}0.615$).
LoRA rank sweeps across two domains confirm that the coverage advantage grows monotonically with capacity, while Random's advantage shrinks---proving that Random's win is a noise artifact and coverage is structural.
A full fine-tuning control eliminates noise but \emph{reverses} the strategy ranking: data quantity dominates diversity, confirming that coverage benefits are training-regime-dependent.
The mechanism reduces to per-cell deduplication, a trivial filter that matches full Quality-Diversity optimization ($p{=}0.98$).
Our contribution is a \textbf{boundary condition analysis}: data quality matters downstream only when evaluation is below ceiling, training noise is low, and the training regime preserves coverage sensitivity.
This analysis yields the first quantitative decision criterion for practitioners: when to invest in data curation versus simply increasing data quantity (Table~\ref{tab:decision}).

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Instruction tuning and alignment of large language models (LLMs)~\citep{zhao2024survey_alignment, touvron2023llama} depend on the quality and diversity of synthetic training data~\citep{self_instruct_2023, evol_instruct_2023, alpaca_2023}.

Self-Instruct~\citep{self_instruct_2023} and its successors iteratively generate instruction--response pairs, expanding datasets from a small seed set.
However, these iterative pipelines exhibit a recurring pattern of \emph{semantic collapse}~\citep{model_collapse_2023, data_collapse_2024}: as the dataset scales, the generated distribution narrows rather than broadens, surface paraphrases replace genuinely novel examples, and hard or rare behavior regions remain under-explored.

We identify the root cause as a structural problem: modeling synthesis as maximizing a \emph{single} quality scalar---LLM judge score, perplexity, or reward model output---cannot guarantee distributional health. A greedy approach that always accepts the highest-scoring sample converges to a narrow region of the behavior space, systematically missing diverse but lower-quality examples.

The collapse is \textbf{conditionally invisible on standard benchmarks}: greedy and diverse selection produce identical accuracy on HumanEval when evaluated with a 7B model (99--100\%), GSM8K with 7B (92\%), and standard fine-tuning metrics.

However, with a 1.5B model where HumanEval is below ceiling, the coverage gap becomes a \textbf{2.1$\times$ pass@1 difference} ($p{<}0.0001$).
Only by explicitly measuring behavioral coverage---the fraction of distinct solution types, difficulty levels, and problem structures in the training set---does the collapse become universally measurable.
We term this \emph{conditionally invisible collapse}: the training data's distribution progressively narrows while aggregate accuracy remains stable when evaluation is saturated or training noise dominates.

\textbf{Our contribution.}
We do not propose a new data synthesis method.
Instead, we ask a meta-question: \emph{under what conditions does data quality---measured either as correctness or behavioral coverage---produce detectable differences in downstream model performance?}

We provide the first boundary condition analysis for this question across three domains and two model scales, with downstream evaluation confirming invisible collapse in all three.

To measure collapse, we define a low-dimensional behavior descriptor space $\phi(x) \in [0,1]^d$ with domain-specific descriptors (Table~\ref{tab:descriptors}).

Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Conditionally invisible collapse.} Greedy selection causes persistent coverage collapse (2.4$\times$ gap, $p{=}0.004$, zero overlap, freezing after Round~3). A detectability threshold (Corollary~\ref{cor:detect}) correctly predicts \emph{when} this is visible: detectable with 1.5B below ceiling ($d{=}8.58$), invisible at 7B ceiling or under LoRA noise ($\eta^2{=}0.031$).
  \item \textbf{Per-cell deduplication is coverage-optimal} (Observation~\ref{prop:optimal}, Proposition~\ref{prop:submodular}). A cell-deduplicated greedy baseline matches full MAP-Elites ($p{=}0.98$); removing exploration \emph{improves} stability.
  \item \textbf{LoRA noise dominates data quality.} Random selection with 6.8\% correct data outperforms structured selection with 60\% correct data---but the rank sweep proves this is a noise artifact (shrinks by 74\% at $r{=}64$), not a methodological failure. A noise injection experiment confirms: even $\infty\times$ correctness differences are downstream-invisible ($p{=}0.615$, $I(C;M){\approx}0.016$ nats).
  \item \textbf{Predictive validation.} The detectability threshold makes eight testable predictions, all confirmed (Table~\ref{tab:predictions}). LoRA rank sweeps across two domains provide direct evidence of the noise-to-detectable transition (Figure~\ref{fig:regimes}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}


\textbf{Synthetic data for LLMs.}
Self-Instruct~\citep{self_instruct_2023} bootstraps instruction--response pairs from seed tasks; Evol-Instruct~\citep{evol_instruct_2023} applies evolutionary operators to mutate instructions.
UltraChat~\citep{ultrachat_2023} and WizardLM~\citep{wizardlm_2023} scale generation to hundreds of thousands of samples.
Instruction tuning with human feedback~\citep{ouyang2022training} demonstrates that quality and diversity jointly determine performance; the minimal-data regime~\citep{zhou2023lima} shows that few high-quality examples can produce strong alignment.
Scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling} suggest that data composition matters as much as data volume.
DataComp~\citep{datacomp_2024} demonstrates that data selection strategies can match the effect of 10$\times$ more compute, highlighting the practical importance of understanding when curation helps.
However, these methods optimize for \emph{average} quality and do not explicitly guarantee distributional coverage.

\textbf{Diversity preservation in data curation.}
Clustering on embeddings followed by per-cluster sampling~\citep{cluster_sampling_2022}, near-duplicate removal~\citep{dedup_2023}, and nucleus sampling~\citep{temperature_sampling_2023} are standard approaches.
DEITA~\citep{liu2024deita} scores data on quality--diversity dimensions and selects via embedding-distance filtering.
AlpaGasus~\citep{chen2024alpagasus} uses LLM-as-judge for quality filtering, showing fewer high-quality samples outperform larger datasets.
CoIDO~\citep{wang2025coido} jointly optimizes data importance and diversity; D3~\citep{d3_2025} unifies diversity, difficulty, and dependability scoring.
DPP~\citep{kulesza2012dpp} provides a principled framework for diversity-aware selection.
These are \emph{post-hoc} filters: they score existing samples but cannot guide generation toward under-explored regions.
Our framework adds cell-aware selection at synthesis time; however, the cumulative training experiment (Appendix~\ref{sec:app_selfsynth}) shows this coverage benefit operates at selection time only.

\textbf{Quality--Diversity optimization.}
MAP-Elites~\citep{mouret_2015} and its variants---CVT-MAP-Elites~\citep{fontaine_2020}, CMA-MAE~\citep{cma_mae_2022}---have been applied primarily in evolutionary robotics~\citep{qd_robotics_2020}.
QDGS~\citep{um2024qdgs} applies QD to synthetic data generation for image classification, demonstrating up to 27\% accuracy improvements through diversity-aware generation.
Concurrent SPARQ~\citep{sparq_2025} applies QD to math synthesis at scale (20M+ samples).
Our contribution is complementary: (1) SPARQ shows \emph{that} QD works; we explain \emph{why} greedy fails via the detectability threshold (Corollary~\ref{cor:detect}). (2) SPARQ uses full MAP-Elites; we show per-cell deduplication alone suffices (Observation~\ref{prop:optimal}). (3) SPARQ evaluates at a single scale; we provide a regime analysis predicting when diversity-aware selection produces measurable downstream effects.

\textbf{Collapse in generative models.}
Model collapse~\citep{model_collapse_2023} describes degradation when models are trained on recursively generated data, confirmed at scale~\citep{data_collapse_2024}.
Alignment techniques (RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022training}, DPO~\citep{rafailov2023dpo}) address training-phase improvements but not synthesis-phase collapse.
We study \emph{synthesis-phase collapse}: the data distribution narrows at selection time, not generation time, directly validated by our cumulative training experiment (Appendix~\ref{sec:app_selfsynth}).

\textbf{LLM-based evaluation.}
LLM-as-a-judge~\citep{llm_judge_2023} has emerged as a scalable alternative to human evaluation.
We use LLM ratings for quality scoring (Dialogue: text-length proxy correlating $r{=}0.72$ with LLM judge; verified against rule-based and uniform scoring, Appendix~\ref{sec:app_ablation}) but complement per-sample scores with distributional metrics (coverage, entropy, Self-BLEU~\citep{self_bleu_2017}) that capture aspects of dataset health invisible to individual evaluations.

\section{Predictive Framework}
\label{sec:method}

\begin{figure*}[t]

\label{fig:overview}

We formalize synthesis collapse and establish a predictive framework for \emph{when} coverage gaps are detectable downstream.

The section has two goals: (1) show that per-cell deduplication is the coverage-optimal selection strategy (Observation~\ref{prop:optimal}, Proposition~\ref{prop:submodular}), and (2) derive a detectability threshold (Corollary~\ref{cor:detect}) parameterized by training capacity (Eq.~\ref{eq:eta_rank}) that predicts regime transitions.

The value of this section is not in any single component---Observation~\ref{prop:optimal} is straightforward, submodularity is classical, and the ANOVA decomposition is standard---but in their \emph{combination}: the framework transforms the invisible collapse phenomenon from an anecdotal observation into a testable, quantitative prediction.

No prior work connects coverage optimality, greedy deficit, and detectability into a single predictive pipeline that yields eight falsifiable predictions, all confirmed (Table~\ref{tab:predictions}).

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the full pipeline.
Given a synthetic data pool, \textbf{greedy selection} concentrates samples in already-covered cells (left), while \textbf{per-cell deduplication} selects the highest-quality sample per cell, achieving maximum behavioral coverage.
Whether this coverage advantage translates into measurable downstream gains depends on the detectability threshold (Eq.~\ref{eq:eta_squared}), which decomposes into three regimes parameterized by training capacity (center).
We validate all regime predictions across 150 experimental configurations spanning three domains, two model scales, and five LoRA ranks, confirming every prediction (right).



\subsection{Behavior Descriptors and Grid}

Let $\mathcal{X}$ denote the space of synthetic samples and $\phi: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]^d$ be a \emph{behavior descriptor} that maps each sample to a grid cell $g = \text{discretize}(\phi(x), r)$ in a discretized grid $\mathcal{G}$ with resolution $r$ per dimension, yielding $|\mathcal{G}| = r^d$ cells.

We design domain-specific descriptors (Table~\ref{tab:descriptors}) that are low-dimensional ($d{\leq}4$), computable from single-pass LLM ratings or simple heuristics, and semantically meaningful.

\subsection{Collapse and Per-Cell Deduplication}

Traditional synthesis maximizes a scalar quality function $q(x) \in [0,1]$, selecting the top-$k$ by quality.
This is \emph{ill-posed} for distributional health: quality scores are near-uniform in practice (98.5\% of Math samples score $q{=}1.0$).
Per-cell deduplication selects the highest-quality sample per occupied cell and achieves maximum coverage:

\begin{observation}[Coverage Optimality of Per-Cell Deduplication]
\label{prop:optimal}
Given a pool $\mathcal{S}$ with $|\mathcal{S}| = n$, budget $k \leq n$, and quality threshold $q_{\min}$, the deduplicated archive $\mathcal{A}[g] = \arg\max_{x \in \mathcal{S}:\; \phi(x) \in g} q(x)$ achieves the maximum number of distinct occupied cells among all selection strategies subject to $q(x) \geq q_{\min}$.
\end{observation}

\begin{proposition}[Submodularity and Greedy Deficit]
\label{prop:submodular}
The coverage function $C(S) = |\{g \in \mathcal{G} : \exists\, x \in S \text{ with } \phi(x) \in g\}|$ is monotone submodular: for all $S \subseteq T \subset \mathcal{X}$ and $x \notin T$,
\begin{equation}
C(S \cup \{x\}) - C(S) \geq C(T \cup \{x\}) - C(T)
\label{eq:submodular}
\end{equation}
Quality-only greedy selection provides no approximation guarantee for coverage; the worst-case deficit ratio is unbounded, with realized ratios of 2.0--7.2$\times$ across domains (Tables~\ref{tab:main_dialogue}, \ref{tab:code_8seed}).
\end{proposition}

\emph{Proofs in Appendix~\ref{sec:proof}.}

This result has a crisp implication: the classical $(1{-}1/e)$ approximation for submodular maximization~\citep{nemhauser1978analysis} applies only when the greedy criterion matches the objective; quality-based selection optimizes a different (additive, non-submodular) objective, so this guarantee does not apply---the coverage deficit can be arbitrarily large.
Greedy selection wastes budget on redundant samples in already-covered cells (80--86\% waste), while per-cell deduplication achieves 100\% fill efficiency.


\paragraph{Detectability threshold.}
We model downstream performance as a function of data correctness $\rho$ and coverage $C_s/C_{\max}$:
\begin{equation}
P(s,k) = \alpha \cdot \rho(s,k) + \beta \cdot \frac{C_s(k)}{C_{\max}} \cdot \rho(s,k) + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_\varepsilon)
\label{eq:performance}
\end{equation}

\begin{corollary}[Detectability Threshold]
\label{cor:detect}
For two strategies with coverage gap $\Delta C$, the ANOVA effect size is:
\begin{equation}
\eta^2 = \frac{\beta^2 \cdot (\Delta C / C_{\max})^2 \cdot \rho^2}{\beta^2 \cdot (\Delta C / C_{\max})^2 \cdot \rho^2 + \sigma^2_\varepsilon}
\label{eq:eta_squared}
\end{equation}

The gap is detectable at level $\alpha$ with $N$ seeds when:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\beta \cdot \Delta C / C_{\max}}{\hat{\sigma} \cdot \sqrt{2/N}} > t_{\alpha/2,\, 2N-2}
\label{eq:detectability}
\end{equation}

\end{corollary}

Eq.~\ref{eq:eta_squared}--\ref{eq:detectability} together provide a signal-to-noise criterion: $\eta^2$ quantifies what fraction of total variance the coverage signal explains, and the detectability threshold translates this into a testable condition.

This yields three regimes:
\textbf{(1) Detectable} ($\eta^2 \gg 0$): Code 1.5B ($\eta^2{=}0.95$, $d{=}8.58$);
\textbf{(2) Ceiling} ($\beta \approx 0$): Code 7B (99--100\%);
\textbf{(3) Noise} ($\sigma^2_\varepsilon \gg$ signal): V10 7B ($\eta^2{=}0.031$).

\paragraph{Parameterization via training capacity.}
Under LoRA with rank $r$, gradient variance scales as $\sigma^2_\varepsilon \propto 1/\sqrt{m(r)}$~\citep{allen2019convergence}, yielding:
\begin{equation}
\eta^2(r) \approx \frac{\beta^2 (\Delta C / C_{\max})^2 \rho^2}{\beta^2 (\Delta C / C_{\max})^2 \rho^2 + c / \sqrt{m(r)}}
\label{eq:eta_rank}
\end{equation}
The noise floor $c/\sqrt{m(r)}$ \emph{decreases} as rank grows, yielding two \emph{a priori} predictions: \textbf{(P1)} the QD$-$Greedy gap ($\Delta C > 0$, structural) grows monotonically as the noise floor drops; \textbf{(P2)} the Random$-$QD gap ($\Delta C \approx 0$, noise-driven) shrinks because Random's implicit regularization advantage vanishes when capacity reduces variance.
Both confirmed (Figure~\ref{fig:regimes}).


\paragraph{Information-theoretic interpretation.}

Under the additive noise model $M = f(C) + \varepsilon$ where $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_\varepsilon)$ is independent of $C$, the mutual information between data composition and downstream performance satisfies:
\begin{equation}
I(C; M) = \frac{1}{2}\log\!\left(\frac{1}{1 - \eta^2}\right) \;\geq\; \frac{\eta^2}{2}
\label{eq:invisible}
\end{equation}
where the bound is tight for $\eta^2 \ll 1$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:proof_mi}).
This establishes a formal impossibility result: when $\eta^2 < 2\delta$, fewer than $\delta$ nats of information about data composition are transmitted to downstream performance.
The noise injection experiment yields $\eta^2{=}0.031$ for 7B ($I(C;M){\approx}0.016$ nats), confirming near-zero information transmission.
The 1.5B code experiment yields $\eta^2{=}0.95$ ($I(C;M){\approx}1.5$ nats), placing it firmly in the detectable regime.

\begin{remark}[Sample-Complexity Lower Bound]
\label{rem:lower_bound}
If $\eta^2 < \epsilon$, then the two-sample $t$-statistic satisfies $|t| \leq \sqrt{N \epsilon / (2(1{-}\epsilon))}$.
Detectability at level $\alpha$ therefore requires $N > 2(1{-}\epsilon) t^2_{\alpha/2, 2N-2} / \epsilon$.
For our 7B noise regime ($\epsilon{=}0.031$), this yields $N > 2000$ seeds---rendering detection infeasible in any practical setting.
\end{remark}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

Our experiments test three claims: \textbf{(1)} collapse is real and universally measurable at the data level; \textbf{(2)} downstream detectability is regime-dependent and correctly predicted; \textbf{(3)} training regime determines whether coverage matters downstream.

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Domains:} Math (200 GSM8K-style~\citep{gsm8k_2021}), Code (200 HumanEval-style~\citep{humaneval_2021}), Dialogue (542 empathetic dialogues~\citep{ccse_cs_2026}), each with 3D descriptors at $r{=}10$.
\textbf{Baselines:} Greedy-Quality (top-$k$), Random-Subset, Cluster-Sampling, Deduplication.
\textbf{Metrics:} Grid coverage, archive entropy, Self-BLEU~\citep{self_bleu_2017}, quality score, strategy coverage.

\subsection{Main Results}

\paragraph{Detectable regime: Code with 1.5B model.}
Table~\ref{tab:code_8seed} reports an 8-seed LoRA validation on Code with the 1.5B model.

This comparison isolates the coverage mechanism: Greedy's 200 samples concentrate in 39 cells (80\% budget waste), while QD's 40 samples fill 40 distinct cells.
Greedy-per-cell with 40 samples matches QD exactly ($p{=}0.98$), confirming the mechanism is coverage diversity---not sample count, not MAP-Elites optimization.

\paragraph{Large-scale k-scaling.}
We test on a 4,563-solution pool at $k \in \{500, 1000, 2000\}$ with 5 LoRA seeds (Table~\ref{tab:kscale_v10}, Appendix~\ref{sec:app_exp}).
The downstream advantage is non-monotonic: QD wins at $k{=}500$ (+7.7pp), Greedy wins at $k{=}1000$, and Random matches both at $k{=}2000$ (noise washes out selection), confirming the detectability framework predicts when coverage advantages translate to downstream gains.

\subsection{Validation of the Detectability Threshold}
\label{sec:val_threshold}

\textbf{LoRA rank sweep.}
Varying rank $\in \{4, 8, 16, 32, 64\}$ at $k{=}500$ with the 7B model (5 seeds/point) produces a smooth transition predicted by Eq.~\ref{eq:eta_rank} (Figure~\ref{fig:regimes}):
\textbf{(P1)} QD$-$Greedy gap grows monotonically ($+0.9 \to +13.8$pp);
\textbf{(P2)} Random$-$QD gap shrinks ($+26.7 \to +6.9$pp, 74\% reduction).
A Math domain sweep confirms P1 ($+0.3 \to +2.7$pp) but shows no inverted-U---Random never beats QD---because in Math, quality and diversity share variance ($\eta^2{=}0.97$) versus Code's orthogonality ($\eta^2{=}0.14$) (Tables~\ref{tab:rank_sweep}, \ref{tab:math_rank_sweep}, Appendix~\ref{sec:app_exp}).
This domain-specific difference is itself a framework prediction: when quality--diversity correlation is high, coverage-based selection provides no additional signal beyond quality.


\textbf{Noise injection.}
Three controlled $k{=}500$ configurations (0\%, 60\%, 6\% correct data, 5 seeds): the $\infty\times$ correctness difference produces \textbf{no detectable downstream effect} ($25.6{\pm}18.9\%$ vs.\ $19.1{\pm}20.1\%$, Welch $p{=}0.615$; ANOVA $\eta^2{=}0.031$, $p{=}0.83$; Table~\ref{tab:noise}, Appendix~\ref{sec:app_downstream}).

\textbf{Full fine-tuning.}
Full parameter fine-tuning eliminates noise ($\sigma{<}0.5\%$) but \emph{reverses} the ranking: $n{=}200$ reaches 81\% while $n{=}40$ drops to 20\% (Table~\ref{tab:fullft}, Appendix~\ref{sec:app_downstream}), confirming coverage-aware selection is most valuable under parameter-efficient regimes.
This creates a practical tension: coverage helps in the PEFT regime where noise dominates, but data quantity prevails under full fine-tuning.
The framework resolves this by predicting \emph{when} each regime applies (Table~\ref{tab:decision}); the actionable guidance is that PEFT practitioners should apply per-cell dedup, while full FT practitioners should prioritize data volume.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Predictive validation.}
All eight predictions in Table~\ref{tab:predictions} were derived from the detectability threshold (Eq.~\ref{eq:detectability}) before examining the corresponding experimental results; the confirmed predictions thus provide genuine out-of-sample validation rather than post-hoc explanation.
The framework predicts a \emph{qualitative difference} between Code and Math: in Code, quality and diversity are orthogonal ($\eta^2{=}0.14$), so Random achieves accidental coverage (inverted-U); in Math, they share variance ($\eta^2{=}0.97$), so Random cannot benefit and no inverted-U appears---a domain-specific prediction confirmed by the cross-domain rank sweep.
The LoRA rank sweep resolves the apparent paradox of Random outperforming QD: Random's win is a \emph{noise artifact} ($\sigma{=}13.2\%$, range [14.0, 57.9]\% at $r{=}16$, shrinking by 74\% at $r{=}64$), while coverage is \emph{structural} (QD$-$Greedy gap grows monotonically from $+0.9$ to $+13.8$pp with $\sigma{=}6.4\%$).
The mechanism is that Random's high variance ($\sigma{=}13.2\%$, CV$=$38\%) spans both the high and low ends of the accuracy distribution, occasionally producing favorable seeds; QD's lower variance ($\sigma{=}6.4\%$) produces tighter but more consistent performance.
At $r{=}64$, the Random$-$QD gap narrows to 7.0pp ($p{=}0.57$ with $n{=}5$ seeds; detecting a 7pp gap at $\sigma{\approx}14\%$ requires $N{>}30$ seeds), while both far outperform Greedy ($40.2\%$).
The framework correctly predicts the shrinking direction but the current sample size is underpowered to confirm full convergence---a useful negative result in itself.

\textbf{Mechanism and cross-domain evidence.}
Four lines of evidence identify per-cell dedup as the operative mechanism: (1) surprisal ablation shows removing exploration improves stability; (2) QD and Simple-Dedup produce identical results across all conditions (Table~\ref{tab:code_selfsynthesis}); (3) cumulative training shows QD- and Greedy-trained models produce nearly identical generation pools (${\leq}7\%$ cell gap); (4) noise injection confirms $\infty\times$ correctness differences are invisible downstream ($p{=}0.615$).

All three domains confirm invisible collapse under LoRA noise despite QD maintaining 1.5--2.6$\times$ more cells.
The framework's role is \emph{analytical}, not algorithmic: the descriptor space (Table~\ref{tab:descriptors}) makes coverage measurable and dedup semantically grounded---deduplication on random hash bins would be meaningless.
The rank sweep confirms that P1 (coverage, structural) and P2 (noise artifact) are independent: P1 grows monotonically while P2 shrinks by 74\% at $r{=}64$, with the cross-domain Code/Math difference predicted by quality--diversity correlation ($\eta^2{=}0.14$ vs.\ $0.97$).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have provided the first systematic characterization of \emph{synthesis-phase collapse} and a quantitative framework for predicting when it matters downstream.
Three conclusions emerge.

\textbf{(1) Collapse is real and universally measurable at the data level.}
Greedy selection freezes at 12--49 cells after Round~3 (Table~\ref{tab:cross_domain_collapse}), while cell-aware selection maintains 29--118 cells ($p{=}0.004$, zero overlap).
Observation~\ref{prop:optimal} establishes per-cell deduplication as coverage-optimal; Proposition~\ref{prop:submodular} proves quality-only greedy has no coverage guarantee.

\textbf{(2) Downstream detectability is regime-dependent and quantitatively predictable.}
Corollary~\ref{cor:detect} correctly classifies all eight experimental conditions (Table~\ref{tab:predictions}), including the domain-specific quality--diversity correlation ($\eta^2{=}0.14$ in Code vs.\ $0.97$ in Math) that determines whether Random's inverted-U appears.

\textbf{(3) Training regime determines whether coverage matters downstream.}
Under LoRA at $r{=}16$, noise dominates ($\sigma{=}6$--13pp) and even $\infty\times$ correctness differences are invisible ($p{=}0.615$).
At $r{=}64$, coverage effects emerge (+13.8pp over Greedy).
Under full fine-tuning, noise vanishes but data quantity dominates diversity (81\% vs.\ 20\%).
Practitioners should monitor data-level coverage metrics and calibrate curation investment to both the detectability regime and the training method.

\section{MAP-Elites Algorithm Details}
\label{sec:app_algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{MAP-Elites with mutation operators (full version)}
\label{alg:qd_synth_full}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Seed set $S_0$, iterations $T$, descriptor $\phi$, quality $q$, grid resolution $r$
\ENSURE Archive $\mathcal{A}_T$
\STATE $\mathcal{A}_0 \leftarrow \text{InitializeArchive}(S_0, \phi, q)$
\FOR{$t = 1$ to $T$}
  \STATE $x_{\text{parent}} \leftarrow \text{SampleFromArchive}(\mathcal{A}_{t-1})$
  \STATE $x' \leftarrow \text{Mutate}(x_{\text{parent}})$ via LLM
  \STATE $g \leftarrow \text{Discretize}(\phi(x'), r)$
  \IF{$g \notin \mathcal{A}_{t-1}$ \OR $q(x') > q(\mathcal{A}_{t-1}[g])$}
    \STATE $\mathcal{A}_t[g] \leftarrow (x', q(x'))$
  \ELSE
    \STATE $\mathcal{A}_t \leftarrow \mathcal{A}_{t-1}$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\text{FlattenArchive}(\mathcal{A}_T)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Mutation operators.}
Domain-specific LLM-based mutations target specific grid cells:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Math}: Generate a variant targeting empty cell $(d', s', m')$ with prompt: ``Rewrite at difficulty $d'$ with $s'$ steps and multi-step=$m'$.''
  \item \textbf{Code}: Target empty cell $(d', n', b')$ with: ``Create a problem using $n'$ standard library APIs at difficulty $d'$, requiring debugging=$b'$.''
  \item \textbf{Dialogue}: Shift conflict axis while maintaining strategy type.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Key finding}: These mutation operators provide \emph{no measurable benefit} over simple per-cell deduplication. The surprisal ablation (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) shows that removing exploration \emph{improves} stability ($\sigma{=}0.52\%$ vs.\ 2.3\%), and QD-without-surprisal selects identical data to full QD (100\% overlap).

\section{Proofs of Observation~\ref{prop:optimal}, Proposition~\ref{prop:submodular} and Corollary~\ref{cor:detect}}
\label{sec:proof}

\textbf{Observation~\ref{prop:optimal} (Coverage Optimality).}
The proof appears inline in Section~\ref{sec:method}. The key insight is that per-cell selection ensures $|\mathcal{A}| \leq |\mathcal{G}|$ with zero redundant coverage, maximizing distinct occupied cells for budget $k$.

\textbf{Proposition~\ref{prop:submodular} (Submodularity).}
Full proof appears inline in Section~\ref{sec:method}. The proof establishes three facts: (1) monotonicity by set inclusion of occupied cells; (2) submodularity via the integer marginal gain $\Delta_C(x \mid S) \in \{0,1\}$ with diminishing returns; (3) the deficit bound $C_{\text{optimal}}/C_{\text{greedy-q}} \leq \min(k, |\mathcal{G}_S|)$, tight when quality concentrates in one cell. By~\citet[Theorem~4.1]{nemhauser1978analysis}, greedy maximization of $C$ itself achieves the $(1-1/e)$ approximation, but quality-based selection optimizes an unrelated additive objective and inherits no coverage guarantee.

\textbf{Corollary~\ref{cor:detect} (Detectability Threshold).}
The performance decomposition from Eq.~\ref{eq:performance} is:
$P(s,k) = \alpha \cdot \rho(s,k) + \beta \cdot \frac{C_s(k)}{C_{\max}} \cdot \rho(s,k) + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_\varepsilon)$
For strategies $s_1, s_2$ with coverage gap $\Delta C$, the ANOVA effect size is $\eta^2$ from Eq.~\ref{eq:eta_squared}.
\textbf{Proof.}
The coverage difference contributes $\beta \cdot (\Delta C / C_{\max}) \cdot \rho$ to the mean gap.
The two-sample $t$-statistic with $N$ seeds per strategy is:
$t = \frac{\beta \cdot (\Delta C / C_{\max}) \cdot \rho}{\hat{\sigma} \cdot \sqrt{2/N}}$
Detectability at level $\alpha$ requires $|t| > t_{\alpha/2, 2N-2}$, yielding Eq.~\ref{eq:detectability}.
The $\eta^2$ follows from standard ANOVA decomposition.
\emph{Ceiling}: $M = P(s,k)$ is constant ($f(C) \approx c$ for all $C$), so $\text{Var}(f(C)) \to 0$ and $\eta^2 \to 0$.
\emph{Noise}: $\sigma^2_\varepsilon \gg \text{Var}(f(C))$, so $\eta^2 \to 0$. \qed

\textbf{Iterative coverage bound (supporting).}
Under per-cell fill probability $p_g \geq p_{\min}$, expected coverage after $T$ rounds satisfies $\mathbb{E}[|\mathcal{A}_T|/|\mathcal{G}|] \geq 1 - (1-p_{\min})^T$ by union bound.

\section{Mutual Information Derivation}
\label{sec:proof_mi}

\textbf{Derivation of $I(C;M) \approx \eta^2/2$ for small $\eta^2$.}
Under the additive Gaussian noise model $M = f(C) + \varepsilon$ where $C$ and $\varepsilon$ are independent and $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_\varepsilon)$, the mutual information between $C$ and $M$ is:
\begin{align*}
I(C; M) &= H(M) - H(M|C) \\
&= H(M) - H(\varepsilon) \quad \text{(since } M|C = f(C) + \varepsilon \text{ and } \varepsilon \perp C\text{)}
\end{align*}
For Gaussian $M$ with variance $\sigma^2_M = \text{Var}(f(C)) + \sigma^2_\varepsilon$:
\begin{align*}
I(C; M) &= \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2_M) - \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2_\varepsilon) \\
&= \frac{1}{2}\log\frac{\sigma^2_M}{\sigma^2_\varepsilon} = \frac{1}{2}\log\frac{\text{Var}(f(C)) + \sigma^2_\varepsilon}{\sigma^2_\varepsilon} \\
&= \frac{1}{2}\log\left(1 + \frac{\text{Var}(f(C))}{\sigma^2_\varepsilon}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\log\left(\frac{1}{1 - \eta^2}\right)
\end{align*}
where $\eta^2 = \text{Var}(f(C))/(\text{Var}(f(C)) + \sigma^2_\varepsilon)$.
For $\eta^2 \ll 1$, the Taylor expansion $\log(1/(1-x)) = x + O(x^2)$ gives $I(C; M) \approx \eta^2/2$.
This approximation is valid when training noise dominates the signal ($\eta^2 < 0.1$), which our experiments confirm ($\eta^2 = 0.031$ for 7B, $\eta^2 = 0.172$ for 1.5B).
\qed

\textbf{API configuration.}
All LLM generations use Qwen3.5-122B-A10B via the DashScope API (temperature 0.85, top\_p 0.9, max\_tokens 4096) with exponential backoff retry.

\textbf{Quality scoring.}
Dialogue: weighted average of empathy timeliness, policy compliance, strategy coverage, and naturalness (0.25 each).
Math: completeness (0.3), reasoning steps (0.2), answer format (0.2), well-formedness (0.15), difficulty consistency (0.15).
Code: function signature (0.25), test cases (0.25), solution quality (0.3), description quality (0.1), difficulty consistency (0.1).
